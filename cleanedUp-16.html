FIRST IMPRESSIONS: Start Strong!

Since you’ll be writing under time constraints in Paper 2, it is helpful to have a “go-to” strategy for writing your introduction. Here are the three elements of a good Paper 2 introduction:

  1. a “hook”: grabs your reader’s attention and interest 
  2. a bridge: brief introduction/overview of your two works; related to the question; ties “hook” to thesis 
  3. a thesis* statement that links both works and provides an idea of how they will be explored in relation to each other in response to the selected question.

*You will need to have written your thesis statement and created an outline before you compose your introduction. The activities below are designed to follow the “Great Introductions: Part 1” and “Organizing & Outlining Your Paper 2” activities.

  1. The “hook”: its job is to engage the reader from the very first sentence and set the stage for the focus at hand. This means that your hook shouldn’t just sound nice; it should also lead logically into #2. 
  2. Your “bridge” (brief overview of each work) is important for two reasons: 1) your examiner may need some reminders about the content of your chosen work (examiners read and teach a lot of different works, so it may have been years since they’ve read your selection!); and 2) it should link to the Paper 2 question. In other words, in a few sentences, you want to refresh your reader’s memory regarding the two works and do so in a way that clearly relates to the topic at hand. 

Quick tip for great introductions: THINK IN TERMS OF CONCEPTS!

The IB Program encourages conceptual thinking because it leads to new insights, creative innovation, and independent reasoning. When it comes to literary study, broad concepts can provide a “way in” to a text or pair of texts and help you connect literature to real life. After all, we read literature in order to learn more about ourselves, others, and the world around us, and – hopefully – to become better human beings as a result. Thus, if you can think about a work you’re reading in terms of what it might teach us about, say, the importance of perspective, you might better appreciate the work’s real-life significance.

What does this mean for Paper 2?

As you respond to a question, think about it – and your selected works – in relation to one or more of the course concepts. Sometimes, a Paper 2 question will explicitly address a course concept (such as the question, “Often a change of scenery can lead to a change in perspective. Discuss how two of your authors have made use of this idea and to what effect”). Other times, you might need to make the connection. For example, which concepts might relate to, or be illuminated by, this question: “Many works of literature suggest that freedom is often sacrificed for the sake of stability. How do at least two literary works you have studied explore this issue?”

Perhaps you plan to discuss two novels in which young people “come of age” and find their own identity as they seek freedom; or you might discuss two works in which an understanding of what it means to be “free” might be shaped by perspective or communication,, or you might explore the transformation a character undergoes over the course of a story as he makes a choice to relinquish freedom in favour of stability. You might discuss how a writer portrays the idea of freedom through creativity. The list goes on!

PART ONE: 

THE “HOOK”:

EXAMPLE:

Here is the thesis generated by our student in the “Organizing Your Paper 2” activity on the Paper 2 question, “In what ways and to what purpose have two of your writers explored the significance of family (or lack thereof)?”:

While Persepolis is a graphic memoir and Purple Hibiscus a novel, both works share a common concern: the immense impact of family – particularly adult family members –  on a young person’s development. Using techniques such as symbolism and the genre of the Bildungsroman, Satrapi and Adichie explore the ways that families shape our worldviews, occasionally inspire rebellion on the path to self-individuation, and leave lasting impacts on us.

Her hook for an essay with this focus might be something like:

There is an old saying that ”you become the company you keep;” when you’re young, you often spend the most time with your family. As a result, family members often have significant effects on the shaping of a person’s identity, beliefs, and character.

This hook uses an old adage related to the Paper 2 question and introduces the idea of the impact of family on a young person’s development. It will lead logically into her “bridge” section (discussed in PART TWO, below).

YOUR TURN:

  1. Around the room, your instructor will have posted various types of “hooks” you might use in a Paper 2 essay. 
  2. Using the outline (and thesis statement) you created in the Organizing Your Paper 2 and Great Introductions (Part 1: Thesis Statements) activities (on the question about the significance of family), walk to a “hook” idea and compose one for your outline (about 1 - 3 sentences). Note that you are not yet mentioning your works or your thesis – you’re just grabbing the reader’s attention and setting the stage for what’s to come!
  3. Write it on a small piece of paper your instructor will give you and post it under the hook. 
  4. Then, move to a different hook idea; compose and post another hook (on the same outline). 
  5. Once everyone has written two different hooks, get into small groups (2 or 3 people) and perform a gallery walk where you read the posted hooks. Discuss amongst yourselves which hooks seem most effective – are they well-written? Interesting? Logically connected to the prompt?
  6. Select one of the hooks that you read as a group; based on the hook, write what you think this essay will be about (write it on a Post-It note provided). You know the prompt that was used, but can you guess from this hook how the student will be focusing their essay and which two works they have selected?
  7. Groups should walk around again, reading each other’s Post-It notes under the hooks. If you see a Post-It under your hook, see if your peers got the impressions you were hoping for and could guess what was to come!

PART TWO:

THE BRIDGE 

EXAMPLE:

Here again, is the thesis generated by our student in the “Organizing & Outlining Your Paper 2” activity:

While Persepolis is a graphic memoir and Purple Hibiscus a novel, both works share a common concern: the immense impact of family – particularly adult family members –  on a young person’s development. Using techniques such as symbolism and the genre of the Bildungsroman, Satrapi and Adichie explore the ways that families shape our worldviews, occasionally inspire rebellion on the path to self-individuation, and leave lasting impacts on us.

To logically lead up to this thesis while also briefly summarizing The Complete Persepolis and Purple Hibiscus, she might write (after the hook, as shown in Part One):

In her graphic novel The Complete Persepolis, Marjane Satrapi tells of growing up in pre- and post-revolutionary Iran as well as her time attending boarding school alone in Austria. She describes how her childhood, adolescence, and her very identity were shaped by her experiences in these times and places, and her parents, grandmother, and Uncle Anoosh play particularly important roles in her development. In contrast, Chimamanda Adichie’s fictional coming-of-age novel Purple Hibiscus is told by 15-year-old Kambili Achike, who lives in postcolonial Enugu, Nigeria with her father, mother, and brother. Kambili, too, is powerfully shaped by both her setting (at home and at her aunt’s flat) as well as by her family members, especially her parents, her aunt and cousins, and her paternal grandfather Papa-Nnukwu.

Here, the student introduces her two works and their authors and describes them in fairly basic terms but ones which set up the aspects of these works that she’ll discuss in her essay. The details she includes are particularly relevant to the question – “In what ways and to what purpose have two of your writers explored the significance of family (or lack thereof)?”

YOUR TURN:

  1. Using the same outline and thesis statement and one of the hooks you composed above, think about how best to briefly introduce/summarize each of your two works in relation to the focus at hand. What does the reader need to know/remember about each work as it relates to the question? What is not vital to mention here? That is, there is a great deal you could say about each work in terms of summary, themes, literary techniques, etc., but what is going to be most relevant here, and what will lead naturally to your thesis?
  2. Compose 2-3 sentences (total) about your two works. In those sentences, remind your reader what your works are about with the question and your thesis in mind.
  3. Once your sentences are complete, trade with a classmate. Give each other feedback (and revise as necessary).

PART THREE:

CONNECTING THE PIECES

  1. Now, take three separate pieces of paper (each should be about ⅓ of a sheet). On one, write your hook; on the second, write your bridge/overview sentences; on the third, write your thesis. On the back of each, write your initials in small letters (for tracking purposes). 
  2. Each person will bring their three pieces of paper to the front of the room and place it in the appropriate stack (hooks, bridges/overviews, and thesis statements). Your instructor will divide these in thirds or fourths (depending on the size of the class), then shuffle them. 
  3. Get into small groups; each group will be given a stack of hooks, a stack of bridge/overviews, and a stack of thesis statements.
  4. Work together to see if you can “match up” each set and assemble introductory paragraphs that make sense. Hopefully, it will be obvious how each set of three pages comes together to form a logical, organized, coherent introduction! 
  5. This activity should help reveal sections that need some revision in order to cohere. Use this activity to think about your own writing and how better to connect your hook with your overview and then lead to your thesis!

Finally, here is the complete sample student introduction (from the pieces written above and in Part 1 of Great Introductions) – with hook, bridge, and thesis statement colour-coded:

There is an old saying that ”you become the company you keep;” when you’re young, you often spend the most time with your family. As a result, family members often have significant effects on the shaping of a person’s identity, beliefs, and character. In her graphic novel The Complete Persepolis, Marjane Satrapi tells of growing up in pre- and post-revolutionary Iran as well as her time attending boarding school alone in Austria. She describes how her childhood, adolescence, and her very identity were shaped by her experiences in these times and places, and her parents, grandmother, and Uncle Anoosh play particularly important roles in her development. In contrast, Chimamanda Adichie’s fictional coming-of-age novel Purple Hibiscus is told by 15 year-old Kambili Achike, who lives in postcolonial Enugu, Nigeria with her father, mother, and brother. Kambili, too, is powerfully shaped by both her setting (at home and at her aunt’s flat) as well as by her family members, especially her parents, her aunt and cousins, and her paternal grandfather Papa-Nnukwu. While Persepolis is a graphic memoir and Purple Hibiscus a work of fiction, both works share a common concern: the immense impact of family – particularly adult family members –  on a young person’s development. Using techniques such as symbolism and the genre of the Bildungsroman, Satrapi and Adichie explore the ways that families shape our worldviews, occasionally inspire rebellion on the path to self-individuation, and leave lasting impacts on us.
