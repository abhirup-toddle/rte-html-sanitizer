Humanities, social sciences are necessary to inform environmental studies

Nov. 12, 2023 6:40 p.m.

By Michelle Lin

This post was updated Nov. 12 at 8:24p.m.

Bridging the gap between humanities, social sciences, and environmental studies is not as challenging as commonly perceived. Contrary to the belief that environmental issues can only be solved through deep scientific knowledge, the humanities and social sciences play a crucial role. They provide a unique approach to connecting the public with environmental concerns, often missed by the scientific community's complex jargon and methodologies.

These disciplines have a history of driving social change, impossible through science alone. By crafting narratives and offering policy-supporting data, they engage the public on a personal level, making complex environmental issues more relatable.

Political science professor Michael Ross emphasizes the importance of politics in environmental issues, stating, "The solutions are not mysterious... The real problem is politics." His focus is on how cultural, political, and economic factors, especially in the U.S., shape our approach to climate change. Political science, with its data gathering and analysis skills, is vital in this context.

Similarly, humanities deepen our understanding by highlighting how different cultures perceive environmental changes. Ursula Heise, interim director of the Lab for Environmental Narrative Strategies, notes the influence of colonization histories on global perception of ecological crises. These insights are crucial for global understanding, especially in non-western nations and the global south.

Susanna Hecht, a professor at the Brazil Center and Institute of Environmental Studies, points out the role of indigenous cultures in expressing their experiences with climate change through literature and art. These narratives offer perspectives often overlooked in scientific discourse.

Moreover, humanities and social sciences share fundamental research concepts with traditional science, facilitating interdisciplinary collaboration. For instance, both political science and environmental science use data analysis and surveying methods.

Environmental history is another field where the humanities intersect with science. Environmental historians use both scientific data and human records, including indigenous writings in Latin America dating back 4,000 years, to provide a comprehensive view of environmental issues.
